# Jobless graduate: the Australian graduate bottleneck

This article explores where inequality appears in the transition from university into relevant, full-time work in Australia. Instead of arguing that degrees don't work, this project highlights that the transition from studying to stable graduate employment is uneven, as some groups and people from certain disciplines recover quickly, while others face more persistent gaps.

[Website link](https://nichothenacho64.github.io/jobless-graduate/)

## What it shows

The article moves from exploring the Australian labour market context broadly into specific graduate outcomes, showing the following findings:

* More Australians are completing degrees
* Younger workers remain concentrated in lower-skill roles
* Short-term graduate employment outcomes differ significantly across some subgroups
* Some gaps close after several years, while others persist
* Full-time employment gains don't always mean that graduates are working in roles that use their skills and education

## Development process

The data was cleaned from QILT and SEW spreadsheets into chart-ready CSV files with accompanying JSON metadata. This included standardising labels, handling missing and suppressed values, and calculating percentage point gaps.

## Data sources

This project uses public Australian education and labour-market data:

* [QILT Graduate Outcomes Survey (GOS)](https://www.qilt.edu.au/surveys/graduate-outcomes-survey-%28gos%29) — Short-term outcomes measured around 4–6 months after graduation
* [QILT Graduate Outcomes Survey – Longitudinal (GOS-L)](https://www.qilt.edu.au/surveys/graduate-outcomes-survey---longitudinal-(gos-l)) — Medium-term outcomes measured around 3–4 years later
* [ABS Survey of Education and Work (SEW)](https://www.abs.gov.au/statistics/people/education/education-and-work-australia/latest-release) — General information on education and employment in Australia

_Note: This article is explanatory in nature, looking at population-level survey outcomes and does not predict or reflect any individual graduate's chances._
