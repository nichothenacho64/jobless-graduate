Homeless Graduate

The first year after university, mapped.

Every year, Australian students graduate into a gap between what they were promised and what actually happens. The advice they receive for surviving it is almost always personal, but the data tells a different story. The transition from university to full-time work is a structure. It does not treat everyone equally, and it does not break down where most people assume.

Rather than starting with generic career advice, this website uses national graduate and labour market data to explore where the pressure actually is and what changes in the years after graduation.

Key: FTE means full-time employment, and pp means percentage points. Short-term outcomes are measured 4 to 6 months after graduation. Medium-term outcomes are measured 3 to 4 years later.

The graduate market is crowded at the entry point

Since 2016, the number of Australians holding a bachelor's degree or higher has grown by 48%, from roughly 4.6 million to nearly 6.8 million in under a decade. That growth does not mean that graduates are automatically worse off, but it does change the conditions they face when they start looking for work.

The broader labour market still sorts younger workers into lower-skill roles regardless of qualification. Among employed 15 to 24 year-olds, only 12.3% work in the highest skill category, while 64.5% are concentrated in the two lowest tiers. In contrast, 45.0% of workers aged 35-44 work in the highest skill category. This includes all workers, not only graduates, but it shows the structural landscape that new graduates are entering.

That labour market's pressure becomes most pronounced right after graduation. In the latest GOS-L matched cohort series, from 2024, full-time employment was 71.1% when first measured four to six months after graduation. Three to four years later, the same cohort reached 91.1%. The first year employment gap is real and it appears across every available matched cohort from 2020 to 2024. But the newest short-term GOS figures, including the 2024 result, cannot yet be fully tested against medium-term data, because those cohorts have not had enough time to appear in the longitudinal survey. Whether the pattern holds for the most recent graduates remains unanswered.

Who you are changes the bottleneck

If the first year after graduation is a bottleneck, the next question is who gets stuck. The answer is not always what you would expect.

The largest short-term differences appear across home language and disability. Graduates who speak a language other than English at home had a full-time employment rate of 58.1%, compared to 74.5% for English speakers, which is a 16.4 percentage point gap. Graduates who reported a disability sat at 66.8%, an 8.2 point gap from those without.

But other comparisons run against conventional assumptions. External students outperformed internal and mixed-mode students by 9.3 points. Regional and remote graduates outperformed those in metro areas by 8.9 points. The early transition window results in large differences, but not in a simple and predictable way.

Tracking these groups over time adds a second layer. Some gaps behave like delays. The 16.4 point home-language gap narrows to just 0.8 points by the medium term. Non-English-speaking graduates eventually reach similar employment levels, but take longer to get there. The study-mode gap nearly completely closes.

However, others persist. The disability gap only narrows from 8.2 to 5.3 points over three years. Graduates who reported a disability still face a meaningful disadvantage in full-time work at the medium-term mark. The labour market takes most graduates eventually, but not all of them equally.

What you study changes your outcomes

Demographic background is not the only sorting mechanism. The transition story shifts again by field of study. Some disciplines start with weak short-term employment but recover strongly over the following years. Creative Arts graduates begin at just 49.6% full-time employment and reach 81.3% within three to four years. Communications follows a similar arc, climbing from 55.9% to 84.4%.

But getting full-time work is not the same as getting the right work. Work fit here means whether graduates report that their skills and education are being fully used in their current role. When employment gains are plotted against improvements in work fit, the picture gets more complicated.

Creative Arts graduates gain 31.7 percentage points in full-time employment but only 1.4 points in work fit. They find jobs, but not ones that use what they trained for. Agriculture and Environmental Studies graduates gain 20.7 points in employment alongside 14.6 points in fit. Their recovery actually leads them to work that is relevant to their degree.

And some fields go backwards. Pharmacy graduates start with the highest short-term employment rate at 96.2%, but both their employment rate and their work fit decline slightly by the medium term. A strong start doesn't mean that outcomes will remain favourable for graduates.

    It's not just you

    The transition from university into relevant full-time work in Australia is not a smooth path. It is an uneven structure, shaped by who you are and what you studied rather than by how hard you worked or how ready you felt.

    This is not a personal failure story. If it reflects your experience, or the experience of a graduate you know, pass it along. The more people who can see the true nature of this bottleneck, the harder it becomes to treat it as something individuals should just push through.
